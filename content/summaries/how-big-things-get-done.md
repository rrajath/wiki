# How Big Things Get Done

Book by Bent Flyvbjerg and Dan Gardner. Studies why large projects fail and what the successful ones have in common.

## Core argument

Most large projects fail because of two patterns: premature commitment and optimistic estimation. The antidote is a discipline of thinking first, executing second, anchored by historical data.

## Key concepts

**[[Think Slow Act Fast]]** — the successful pattern: invest heavily in upfront planning, resolve unknowns on paper, then execute rapidly with few surprises.

**Think Fast, Act Slow** — the failure pattern: commit quickly under social pressure, discover problems mid-execution, slow down to fight fires.

**[[Commitment Fallacy]]** — the social pressure that drives premature commitment: fear of appearing as the blocker, deference to authority, optimism bias.

**[[Reference Class Forecasting]]** — the empirically grounded estimation method:
1. **[[Reference Class Forecasting#Step 1: Anchor|Anchor]]**: take the average of similar past projects as your baseline estimate.
2. **[[Reference Class Forecasting#Step 2: Adjust|Adjust]]**: modify the anchor based on factors specific to your project.

## The planning paradox

Planning feels like it slows you down. In practice, thorough planning compresses execution time because it eliminates the rework and firefighting that dominate poorly-planned projects. The total time (planning + execution) is shorter, and quality is higher.

## Application at small scale

The framework scales down to individual tasks and daily work. Before starting any significant task, spend time front-loading clarity — what is the goal, what could go wrong, what do I need — then execute without hesitation.
