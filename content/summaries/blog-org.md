# blog.org — Blog Post Drafts & Ideas

A collection of in-progress blog post drafts and topic ideas. 2020–2025.

## Notable drafts (substantive content)

### Decisions vs Outcomes

One of the most developed drafts. Key insight from *Thinking in Bets* by Annie Duke:

Decisions and outcomes are separate things separated by time and variables outside your control. The sequence:
1. Encounter a situation.
2. Make a decision with available information.
3. Time passes; uncontrolled variables play out.
4. Experience the outcome.
5. We conflate "bad outcome" with "bad decision" — often wrongly.

A bad outcome with a good decision is not a failure — given the same information, you would (and should) make the same choice again. Separating decisions from outcomes reduces emotional reactions to results and allows more rational evaluation.

Connection to [[You Always Make the Best Decision]] — both concepts center on the fact that decisions are made with available information; judgment based on later information is unfair.

### One Tool to Rule Them All (anti-pattern)

The trap: finding a universal tool, customizing it to death, then discovering it covers 90% of your needs while taking 150% of your time. The insight: real power comes from using multiple specialized tools and building a workflow that integrates the best of each — not from enforcing one tool everywhere.

Examples: tracking workouts in Emacs vs a fitness app; IDE via lsp-mode vs VSCode.

Connection to [[Easy Plug-and-Play]] and [[Workflow]].

### Lessons from Stardew Valley

There are always more tasks than time. The game makes this viscerally obvious: mining, farming, fishing, foraging, quests — you cannot do all of them in a single day. The lesson: prioritize, commit to the choice, and accept that unchosen tasks wait.

Direct application: [[Prioritization as a Knapsack Problem]].

### Ergo Mechanical Keyboards

Personal journey from Keychron K1 → Moonlander → Corne → Microdox Bud. Interest turned obsession; hands-on building and debugging experience. Connection to [[Skill Stacking]] and the interest-vs-obsession distinction from thoughts.org.
