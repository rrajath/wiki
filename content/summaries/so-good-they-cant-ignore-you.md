# So Good They Can't Ignore You

Book by Cal Newport. Core argument: "follow your passion" is bad advice. Instead, build rare and valuable skills first — passion follows mastery, not the other way around.

## Key ideas

**Adopt the [[Craftsman Mindset]]**, not the [[Passion Mindset]].

- The *passion mindset* asks: "What can the world offer me?" It leads to chronic dissatisfaction because no job fully delivers on its promise.
- The *craftsman mindset* asks: "What can I offer the world?" It is action-oriented and leads to mastery.

**Build [[Career Capital]]** — rare and valuable skills that give you leverage to demand a great career.

- Capital is accumulated through [[Deliberate Practice]]: deliberately stretching your abilities beyond comfort zones and receiving ruthless feedback.
- Simply "showing up and working hard" plateaus quickly without deliberate practice.

**Know your [[Career Capital Market]]**:

- *Winner-take-all* markets have one type of capital everyone competes for (e.g., screenwriting).
- *Auction* markets accept many different capital types — you can build a unique combination.

**[[Make Little Bets]]** — small experiments that test ideas and add to your capital portfolio before committing to a big direction.

## Mental model

```mermaid
flowchart LR
    CM[Craftsman Mindset] --> DP[Deliberate Practice]
    DP --> CC[Career Capital]
    CC --> MLB[Make Little Bets]
    MLB --> CC
    CC --> GC[Great Career]
```

## Why it matters

The book reframes career anxiety: you do not need to discover a pre-existing passion. You create passion by becoming exceptionally good at something valuable.

## Note on Derek Sivers

Newport cites [[entities/Derek Sivers]]' "Law of Financial Viability" — money flows toward execution of ideas people value, not toward ideas in the abstract. This is the same principle as [[Ideas as Multipliers]]: ideas are multipliers; execution is where the value is created.
