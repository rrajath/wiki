# Books Referencing Derek Sivers

Page at [sive.rs/ref](https://sive.rs/ref) — Derek Sivers' curated list of 66 books that cite his work, spanning business, creativity, personal development, and entrepreneurship.

## What the references reveal

Three concepts from Sivers are cited so frequently they function as standalone mental models:

**[[Hell Yeah or No]]** — the most-cited principle. When evaluating opportunities, if your reaction is anything less than "hell yeah!", the answer is no. Featured in works by Seth Godin, Greg McKeown (*Essentialism*), Benjamin Hardy, Tim Ferriss, Ryan Holiday, and dozens more. It is a decision filter that eliminates the mediocre "yes" — the commitment you make out of obligation or mild interest rather than genuine excitement.

**[[Ideas as Multipliers]]** — ideas without execution are worth nothing. Sivers' formulation: idea is a multiplier; execution is the value. A brilliant idea × zero execution = $0. Referenced in *The Millionaire Fastlane* and *So Good They Can't Ignore You* (Cal Newport's "Law of Financial Viability" draws on this).

**[[First Follower]]** — from Sivers' TED talk on movement dynamics. The first follower of a lone dancer is the crucial act: they signal that the behavior is safe to join. Leadership is often about making it safe for others to follow, not about being the originator. Cited in *How Google Works* (Eric Schmidt), *Essentialism*, and multiple leadership books.

## CD Baby as a case study

The famous CD Baby shipping confirmation email — a humorous, personality-filled message describing the fictional heroic journey of a CD to its customer — appears in multiple marketing books (*1-Page Marketing Plan*, *Fortune Cookie Principle*, *Never Lose a Customer Again*). It demonstrates that unexpected delight in routine touchpoints creates word-of-mouth and loyal customers.

## Sufficiency and intentional living

Several books cite Sivers' post-exit choices: selling CD Baby for $22M, donating the proceeds to music education, and living simply. *The Psychology of Money* (Morgan Housel) uses this as an example of freedom-through-frugality rather than wealth-through-accumulation.
