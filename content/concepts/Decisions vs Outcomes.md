# Decisions vs Outcomes

Decisions and outcomes are separate events, separated by time and variables outside your control. Judging a decision by its outcome is a logical error.

## The sequence

1. Encounter a situation.
2. Make a decision with the information available *at that moment*.
3. Time passes. Many variables you do not control play out.
4. Experience the outcome.
5. (Error) Conclude: bad outcome → bad decision.

The error is in step 5. By the time the outcome arrives, you have more information than when you decided. Judging the decision with that later information is unfair and unhelpful.

## Why it matters

Combining decisions and outcomes leads to emotional, outcome-driven evaluations:
- **Good decision, bad outcome**: feel terrible unnecessarily; may abandon a sound strategy.
- **Bad decision, good outcome**: congratulate yourself; repeat a flawed approach.

Separating them leads to:
- Evaluating decisions on the quality of reasoning, not the luck of outcomes.
- Less emotional reaction to bad results.
- Clearer learning: *what information would have changed my decision?*

## Key insight

If you went back in time to the moment of the decision, with only the information you had then, you would (and should) make the same choice again. That is not a failure — that is consistency with a sound decision process.
