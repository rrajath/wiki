# Think Slow, Act Fast

A project execution philosophy from *How Big Things Get Done* (Bent Flyvbjerg): invest heavily in upfront planning and thinking, then execute rapidly once the plan is locked.

## The anti-pattern: Think Fast, Act Slow

Most projects fail because people do the opposite — they rush to commit ([[Commitment Fallacy]]) without thorough planning, then discover problems mid-execution and slow down to fix them. This produces:

- Poor planning and unforeseen blockers.
- Delays from rework and surprises.
- Low output quality even if the project "finishes."

The pressure to not be seen as "the one slowing things down" drives premature commitment.

## The pattern: Think Slow, Act Fast

1. **Think slowly**: Gather all available information about the project. Explore failure modes. Resolve ambiguities on paper before writing a line of code or breaking ground.
2. **Lock the plan**: Once thinking is complete, the unknowns are minimized and confidence is high.
3. **Act fast**: Execute without hesitation. Fewer surprises → faster, higher-quality delivery.

Result: projects complete on time or early, with far fewer unknowns and much greater clarity.

## Historical example

Nikola Tesla reportedly refused to begin building a project until it made complete sense on paper. Only after full mental simulation would he move to implementation — and when he did, it went quickly.

## Application to everyday work

The model scales down: before starting any significant task (coding a feature, cooking a complex meal), spend time front-loading clarity.

- For cooking: read the full recipe, gather all ingredients, understand the sequence before turning on any heat. Result: less confusion, better food.
- For software: design the interface and data model before writing implementation. Result: less rework, cleaner code.

## Connection to estimation

Once the "think slow" phase is complete, [[Reference Class Forecasting]] provides an anchor for how long execution will take, adjusted for known specifics.
