# Decision Fatigue

The degradation of decision quality that results from making too many decisions — or from mental depletion caused by [[Information Overload]].

## Core idea

Decision-making is a cognitive resource. Each decision (including micro-decisions like what to click next, what to read, how to respond) draws from a limited pool. As the pool depletes, subsequent decisions become lower quality: more reactive, more biased toward the easiest option rather than the best one.

[[Information Overload]] accelerates depletion: when the brain is processing a high volume of inputs, the overhead of filtering and evaluating each piece counts as decision load even if no explicit choice is made.

## Symptoms

- Feeling exhausted after passive consumption (social media, email scanning) without a proportional sense of accomplishment.
- Increased pull toward [[Instant Gratification vs Deep Satisfaction|instant gratification]] as higher-quality options feel too effortful.
- Difficulty making decisions that earlier in the day would have felt easy.

## Mitigation

- **Reduce unnecessary decisions**: routine, habits, and defaults reduce the decision load. This is the productive use of [[Our Defaults]].
- **Cap information intake**: [[Handling Information Overload]] keeps the input volume in check.
- **Protect high-decision periods**: schedule cognitively demanding work when the resource pool is full (typically early in the day).
- **[[Awareness]]**: noticing when decision fatigue is setting in is the first step to addressing it rather than doubling down on low-quality inputs.
