# You Always Make the Best Decision

At any given moment, you make the decision your brain believes is best — given available information, current state, and current values. Looking back and judging that decision with later knowledge is comparing against an unfair baseline.

## The argument

Before any decision, the brain rapidly (and often unconsciously) weighs pros and cons and selects what it calculates as the best move forward. The calculation may be wrong — missing information, distorted by bias, constrained by mood — but the *intent* is always "best available option."

This applies at every scale:
- Long-horizon financial planning years into the future.
- Which app to open next.
- What to eat right now.

## Life as a stream of decisions

Life is a vast collection of tiny decisions made every moment. At any moment you are choosing to do one specific thing rather than thousands of alternatives. These choices cascade — each one shapes the context in which the next decision is made.

Seen this way, large outcomes are the aggregate of countless micro-decisions, most of which felt trivial at the time.

## Why this matters

This reframe reduces self-criticism for past decisions. The question is not "why did I make that choice?" but "what information or framing would have led to a better calculation?" That reframe is more productive: it focuses on improving inputs to future decisions rather than condemning past self.

It does not mean decisions are always good — only that they were the brain's best output at that moment. The goal is to improve the inputs: better information, clearer values, less distorted emotional state.
