# Attaching Identity to Outcomes

The tendency to define who you are by what you produce, achieve, or fail at — treating outcomes as evidence of a fixed, permanent self.

## The mechanism

When a project fails, the thought becomes "I failed" rather than "that project didn't succeed." The self gets fused with the outcome.

This creates a fragile self-image because outcomes are partly outside your control — they depend on circumstances, timing, luck, other people. Tying your identity to something that volatile means your sense of self fluctuates with every success and failure.

## The illusion of the static self

Underlying identity-attachment is a belief in a fixed, ideal version of yourself that outcomes either confirm or deny. But [[We Are Not Static]] — mood, energy, knowledge, and values shift constantly. Holding yourself to a frozen ideal is comparing a dynamic reality against a static fiction.

## Labels as a specific case

[[Labels Anchor Us Down]] is identity-attachment applied to categories: calling yourself "a runner," "a developer," or "a stoic" and then feeling obligated to live up to the label forever, even as you change.

## The remedy

[[Accept Reality as It Is]]: see the outcome clearly without inflating it into a statement about who you are. You did something poorly (or well). That says something about that action in that context — not about your permanent worth or identity.
