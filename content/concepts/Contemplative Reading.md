# Contemplative Reading

A slow, deliberate mode of reading where you read a small amount and then pause to think deeply about it — the opposite of fast, consumptive reading.

## The problem with fast reading

Most reading is consumptive: read as much as possible, move on, forget most of it. The to-read list grows faster than reading speed, creating anxiety and encouraging skimming. Retention is poor because there is no processing time.

## Contemplative reading

- Read a few pages (not chapters, not books) on a topic.
- Stop. Think. Let the ideas settle. Connect them to what you already know.
- Continue only when ready.
- May take months to finish a dense book — and that is fine.

## Benefits

- Dramatically better retention: processing time locks ideas into long-term memory.
- Deeper understanding: slow engagement surfaces implications and questions that fast reading misses.
- Less anxiety: "reading everything" is impossible — better to read little and understand deeply.

## Trade-offs

Not appropriate for all reading. For fiction, light non-fiction, or research scanning, speed is fine. Contemplative reading is reserved for books where understanding matters more than coverage.

## Connection to learning approach

Contemplative reading pairs naturally with [[Handling Information Overload]] strategy of "one obsession at a time": read deeply on one topic, let it compound, before moving on.
