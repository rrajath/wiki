# Stoicism

An ancient Greek and Roman philosophy centered on living according to reason, accepting what cannot be changed, and focusing only on what is within one's control.

## Core principles (as captured in notes)

### [[Dichotomy of Control]]

Everything in the world falls into two categories:
- **Things you can control**: your own judgments, intentions, reactions, effort.
- **Things you cannot control**: external events, other people's behavior, outcomes.

Most suffering comes from treating uncontrollable things as if they were controllable — worrying about them, getting angry, attaching expectations. The stoic practice is to clearly identify which category something belongs to, then direct energy only to the controllable.

### [[Accept Reality as It Is]]

Reality does not care about your opinion of it. It is a cascade of cause and effect, with no inherent fairness or justice. Labeling things "unfair" is an emotional response, not a useful description of reality. Accepting reality as it is — not as you wish it were — is the foundation for acting effectively within it.

### We Are Not Static

People change — their moods, energy, values, capabilities shift multiple times a day. Treating yourself or others as a fixed identity is a distortion. This has downstream implications:

- **[[Identity Attached to Outcomes]]**: Attaching your identity to outcomes (a project's success, a label like "runner") creates a fragile self that can be broken by circumstances outside your control. You are not your outputs.
- **[[Labels Anchor Us Down]]**: Labels freeze a dynamic person into a static snapshot, making it hard to grow or change course without feeling like you're betraying yourself.

### The Arbitrary Future State of Supposed Happiness

We often defer happiness to a future state ("I'll be happy when..."). But that state is:
1. Poorly defined — we don't actually know what will make us happy.
2. Unsatisfying when reached — we immediately move the target.

True contentment comes from [[Accept Reality as It Is]] — peace with the present, not anticipation of a better future.
